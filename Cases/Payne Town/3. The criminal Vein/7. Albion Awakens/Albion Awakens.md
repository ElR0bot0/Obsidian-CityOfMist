# Overview
## Book overview
> Something is going down in Whitecliff. Amid rising crime rates and vandalized businesses, a new gang of bikers is tightening its grip on the neighborhood. Strangely, they are backed by the little man on the street, folks who are eager to transform their concrete jungle home into a bucolic, idyllic community. Is this a wholesome new grassroot movement or a dangerous and violent uprising? Behind it all looms a mysterious ruler, a king among kings, adamant to protect his realm against those who would subjugate it. Now you must choose whether to help him rise to the throne or slay him upon the steps...

More than it is a classic case, Albion Awakens is an unfolding event: the rise to power of the Rift of King Arthur, valiant yet deeply wounded. The PCs partake in this event as investigators at first but soon they must evaluate the new king and his two-wheeled knights and make the hard choice of whether to join them or stand against them. As the crew grows more involved with the dealings in Whitecliff, you, the MC, sow doubt in either direction they lean to, enhancing the dilemma.

This case features greater mobility between locations than usual; PCs can go back to talk to an NPC in a previously investigated location and unlock new clues based on the crew’s actions and affiliation.
### A PERSISTENT ADVERSARY
Unlike the Grail Knights of the other veins, who remain hidden (and therefore, protected from the PCs) until the very end, Vince Beaumont waits for no one, instead putting himself in the front lines of his operation.

Despite this, Beaumont remains as unstoppable as the other Grail Knights thanks to his tenacity, planning, and legendary powers. Even if exposed early on thanks to excellent detective work by the players, he will remain a threat throughout the case: if he’s arrested, he will be held at Precinct 63 and a trusted lieutenant will represent him at the town hall meeting; if he is killed, he will be reborn, maybe even in one of the Errants, or in Lancelot, etc.

# Background
## Book background
### THE ONCE AND FUTURE KING
In the early days of the City, Vince Beaumont (King Arthur) lost his daughter to Corbin Payne. Not only did his daughter vanish off the face of the earth (killed by Payne, he assumed) but the very memory of her name was also erased from his mind, because of the Mist. Still, his love for her remained, but now as an unending longing toward a child he could only remember as a distant memory, tormenting him endlessly. In this way, the Fisher King (Payne) amplified Beaumont’s secret pain – his grief and urge for revenge – and transformed him into his unwilling Grail Knight. Beaumont’s legendary grief-fuelled rage drove him to acts of violence as he strove to amass power, seeking to avenge his daughter and kill the Fisher King, with whom he became obsessed, naming him the Crimson King.

Within just a few years, Beaumont became one of the City’s most nefarious crime lords and eventually succeeded in finding and killing Payne. However, thanks to the Grail, Payne was revived, forcing Beaumont into the realization that it was simply beyond his power to mete out justice. He fell victim to despair and eventually was killed by a rival mobster, his cousin.

But that was not the end. Beaumont’s Mythos of King Arthur, upon whose grave the words “Once And Future King” were written (“Rex quondam, Rexque Futurus”), had a curious effect on his death. When the next City resident awakened as the Rift of King Arthur, they immediately regained all of Beaumont’s memories, as if awakening to a past life, and with those memories came his obsession with power and with the Crimson King (via the Dolorous Stroke). This would happen in times of great crisis, when the City most needed a savior.

Whenever Beaumont’s new host died, he ceased to exist; but as he was reborn again and again, he was becoming accustomed to changing bodies and overpowering the host’s personality, turning them almost instantly into Legendaries and rebuilding his criminal empire within shorter and shorter periods of time. This gave him newfound hope that with more time on this earth, he could find a way to defeat his enemy. In his fury, he defiled the hopefulness of the “Once And Future King” Mythos, and turned it into a vessel for his vengeful crusade against Corbin Payne.
### THE TARGET: WHITECLIFF
In his latest reincarnation, Vince Beaumont’s psyche was reformed in Calvin Cyrus, a citizen of Whitecliff, a lower-middle-class neighborhood in the City. Before his awakening, Calvin was a champion of the Concerned Citizens Committee, or CCC, a volunteer body of local residents working to reduce crime in Whitecliff. After his awakening, Vince Beaumont used Calvin’s influence in the CCC to recruit young idealists and enlist them in the war against the Crimson King, dubbing this new gang the Errants (Knights of the Round Table). His plan: to take over the neighborhood and turn it into his base of operations from which he would take over the entire City.

This time, however, Vince Beaumont has another goal. He knows that Corbin Payne cannot be killed by ordinary means and therefore he is seeking legendary means to do so. Having been a powerful figure in the Rift underworld for many years, he is aware of the work of the Gatekeepers and how they suppress all that is magical and powerful. To obtain his legendary weapon against his Crimson King, he intends to turn all of Whitecliff into an Enclave, Albion, where magic would be real. Only this way, he believes, could he ever hope to find a magical power – a hero, a weapon, or an artifact – great enough to match the reviving power of the Grail, and finally avenge his daughter, whose name he cannot recall.
### AT THE BRINK OF A FLASHPOINT
In the past several weeks since his re-awakening, Beaumont has enlisted several dozen Errants and used them to undermine the stability of everyday life in Whitecliff, eroding the public support for the current Whitecliff mayor, Eleonora Ricci (Sleeper), who seems unable to contain the surge. Just in the last few days, the Errants attacked local businesses associated with the mayor, such as the Double Dragon chinese restaurant, and intimidated the owners. Beaumont is also sending them to clear the district of all other gangs, leading to clashes with local street gangs like the Skoonies, in the basketball court. Next, Beaumont will send his Errants to build barricades at the highway exits that lead to Whitecliff, harassing traffic and charging ludicrous tolls but secretly preparing to secede from the City.

All of this he does with the purpose of laying the groundwork for his current persona of “Calvin Cyrus” to take the reins in the next town meeting, vote Ricci out, and become the next mayor.

There’s a single reason for all of this: the more influential Cyrus/Beaumont becomes and the more he is recognized as the rightful leader of Whitecliff, the stronger the Enclave of Albion will become. Already the community garden in Whitecliff is transforming into vast medieval fields, providing the neighborhood with a supply of locally-grown food. Cutting the neighborhood off from the rest of the City with the barricades will help establish it as its own kingdom and push away the Mist, and if necessary, fight off the Gatekeepers until Beaumont is able to complete his quest against the Crimson King

### SEARCHING FOR A CHAMPION
While all this is happening, the experienced Beaumont is expecting and even hoping for interference from local heroes and vigilantes. He is seeking more knights to add to the ranks of the Errants, or even better, foster as champions in the fight against the Crimson King. He is watching the unfolding of his takeover patiently and painstakingly, awaiting the first signs of a valiant heroine or hero. Once they show up, he will test them, sending them on one of two quests: help his Errants build a battle golem at the junkyard or slay a dragon that has emerged from the Albion Enclave and settled in an overgrown ruin. If they prove themselves, they will gain an audience with him at his hideout, his benefactor's lake house, where he will try to recruit them to his cause.

### CONQUEST OF CAMELOT
Beauomnt’s final move would be to march on the most fortified building in the neighborhood – Precinct 63 – and transform it into the beating heart of the Albion Enclave, Camelot, where he will establish his new seat of power. While this would be significantly easier if he is the newly-elected mayor following the town meeting, Beaumont is prepared to take the building by force, combining the power of his Touched Errants, his battle golem, and any local champions he can rally to his cause (Battle of Precinct 63).

### ARE BEAUMONT’S HOSTS STILL THERE?
Beaumont’s condition is a strange synergy between two Mythoi. The Mythos of King Arthur, the once and future king, preserves the sum memories of all Arthur Rifts. Normally, these memories make the next Rift in line wiser and more experienced. The Mythos of the Fisher King and the Dolorous Stroke, however, infects these memories with legendary pain, a pain that overwhelms not only the original Beaumont, but also anyone who absorbs his memories through the Mythos of King Arthur. Indirectly, the Fisher King Mythos “rides” the Arthur Mythos to transmit itself to different Rifts. Neither Mythos changes what it is at its core; its their interaction that creates this unique condition.

When a new Arthur Rift awakens, they inherit the Dolorous Stroke and become overwhelmed by Beaumont’s pain. This pain is not alien to them: all Arthur Rifts presumably want the power to unite their kingdom and push back invaders who have wronged their land, so often the Rifts’ mundane personality also interfaces with the same Dolorous Stroke and secret pain: a desire for power. Each such incarnation melds with Beaumont’s memories and create a slightly different persona.

In the current state, Beaumont’s hosts are overwhelmed by the combined power of their Mythos and the Dolorous Stroke, and have little power over their actions. It is possible for the PCs to defeat Beaumont by empowering the everyday persona of the host, but that should be as difficult as healing the Dolorous Stroke (see page 18).
